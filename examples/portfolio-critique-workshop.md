## Top Takeaways

1. **Recruiters look at exactly 3 things on first pass: years of experience, sponsorship status, domain overlap with the company's product.** Everything else is downstream. (Source: A, former hiring committee at two large tech companies.) For a 400-application week, recruiters write debriefs for ~20, HMs interview 3-5. Cold-application is mostly noise; warm intro from a former colleague / classmate / former boss / direct DM to recruiter or HM is the channel that actually moves.
2. **Senior portfolios are skimmed, not read.** HM reads landing-page domain signals → clicks one project → reads the first half of the case study (background + process match) → rarely scrolls to results. Information density on the landing page matters more than completeness inside any one case.
3. **Packaging is universal and required — but the legitimate ceiling is "what would this work look like with no political constraints and no engineering blockers."** Anything beyond that ceiling is fabrication; anything below it is undersold.
4. **Different career levels show different things.** Junior: craft + speed of learning (the portfolio's own polish matters more than project content). Mid: independent ownership + non-obvious decisions. Senior: domain match + cross-functional impact + decision quality. Staff/Principal: articles, talks, knowledge sharing, mentorship — the portfolio shifts from work artifact to thought leadership.
5. **Information density on the landing page is the single highest-leverage edit.** Wrong domain tags = HM bounces in 8 seconds. Right tags = clicks in. Choosing tag dimensions (industry vs platform vs problem-type) is more art than science; test multiple framings.

## Sessions / Conversations

### Day 1, 10:00 — Opening plenary (B, host)

- **Other party**: B — founder, former hiring committee
- **Key points / insights**:
  - "How would they know" — the gap between portfolio claim and verifiable reality is universal across senior portfolios; presenter survey suggested ~0% of decks are 100% reality-grounded
  - Recruiter funnel math: 400 apps → ~20 debriefs → 3-5 interviews; cold app is the wrong end of the funnel
  - Domain match heuristic: HM looks for product-category overlap before role match
- **Quote worth remembering**: "Before you fix your case studies, fix your tags. The tags are what get you read at all."
- **Backlog spawn**: revisit landing-page tag taxonomy; A/B different framings with peer reviewers

### Day 1, 14:00 — Small-group critique (A, group of 5)

- **Other party**: A and 4 other presenters
- **Key points / insights**:
  - A's heuristic for case study length: "process detail belongs in the second half. If the first half doesn't earn the click, the second half is wasted."
  - Multiple presenters got the same feedback: "your strongest project is buried at position 4 on the landing page" — landing-page ordering is undervalued
  - Result: 3 of 5 presenters committed to reordering case studies before applying again
- **Quote worth remembering**: "Senior HMs don't read your portfolio. They scan it. Optimize for scan."
- **Backlog spawn**: reorder own case studies; demote weakest project from landing page to archive

### Day 2, 11:00 — 1-on-1 with C (career-transition perspective)

- **Other party**: C — mid-level designer, transitioned from a different field 3 years ago
- **Key points / insights**:
  - C said hiring managers in their target domain weighed "narrative coherence" higher than "tool fluency" once past the recruiter screen
  - Specific framing trick: lead with the through-line ("I keep ending up in problems involving X because Y") instead of the linear timeline
  - C's portfolio uses a "what I learned" subsection per case rather than a "results" subsection — recommended for transition stories where impact metrics aren't always in the candidate's hands
- **Quote worth remembering**: "Linear timelines look like a CV in long form. Through-lines look like a person."
- **Backlog spawn**: rewrite About page hook with through-line framing; consider replacing "results" with "what I learned" in transition-era cases
